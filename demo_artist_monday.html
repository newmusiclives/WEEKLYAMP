VOCAL BOOTH — THE STRAW EXERCISE, FROM THE ACTUAL SCIENCE

If you've taken a voice lesson in the last decade, you've probably been told to phonate into a straw. The exercise gets passed around like folklore in the singing community, but the underlying research is real and worth understanding once instead of taking on faith.

The technique is one of a family of semi-occluded vocal tract exercises (SOVTEs), and the foundational research traces back to Ingo Titze, Distinguished Professor of Speech Science and Voice at the University of Utah. Titze has authored more than 500 papers on the subject; his vocology lab at Utah maintains a public-facing introduction video to the technique.

What's actually happening when you phonate through a straw, in plain terms:

  • The narrow exit raises supraglottal pressure (the pressure above the vocal folds), creating back pressure across the glottis.
  • That back pressure increases the inertive reactance of the vocal tract, which feeds energy back to the vocal folds in a way that smooths vibration.
  • The result: your folds vibrate with less medial compression — less force pushing them together — for the same acoustic output.

Translation: you can warm up vigorously, including to high pitch and high subglottal pressure, with materially lower risk to the vocal fold mucosa than you'd run by warming up directly into open phonation. The peer-reviewed literature consistently reports reduced effort and reduced fatigue under SOVTE protocols.

The practical upshot for working artists: the straw warm-up isn't just a folk remedy. It's a measurably safer way to get to the top of your range before a show or a session, and the research is publicly available if you want to read past the YouTube tutorials. Links below.

SONGCRAFT — HOOKTHEORY'S MOST-USED PROGRESSION

If you wanted a single, defensible answer to “what's the most common chord progression in popular music?” the closest thing to one comes out of Hooktheory's database of 70,000+ song analyses. Their public Trends tool lets you statistically interrogate which chords most often follow which, computed via Markov analysis on user-contributed song transcriptions.

The headline finding from Hooktheory's published analysis: the most popular four-chord progression in the database is I – V – vi – IV — in the key of C, that's C – G – Am – F. This is the progression that Axis of Awesome stitched together a decade ago in a famous live medley, and the underlying reason that medley works is that the progression really is the statistical centre of gravity for Western pop.

What's useful for songwriters is not the single progression but the tool itself: you can build any progression in Hooktheory's interface and get back a list of every song in their database that uses it, plus the empirically most-likely next chord based on the corpus. It is the closest thing the field has to a public, queryable map of pop harmony.

Two practical uses:

  • If you've written a verse-chorus that feels generic, paste the progression into the Trends tool. If your sequence is high-frequency, deliberately push one chord one position off the most-likely path — that single substitution is often the difference between “heard this before” and “hooks me.”
  • If you're stuck on a bridge, query the chord that ends your chorus and let the most-probable next-chord list seed your bridge starting point. You're not copying anything — you're using empirical data to break out of writer's lock.

GEAR GARAGE — TWO MICS THAT ACTUALLY MOVED THE MARKET

Gear coverage in 2026 is mostly noise. Two studio mics released in the past 18 months have, however, genuinely shifted what a working artist can pull off in a project room.

RØDE NT1 5th Generation — $250

Self-noise figure of 4 dBA. RØDE bills it as the world's quietest studio condenser, and reviewers have not seriously contested the claim. Two features make this mic interesting beyond the noise spec: it has both XLR and USB-C output simultaneously via RØDE's Dual Connect design, and over USB it can record in 32-bit float, which means clipping is functionally a non-issue — you adjust gain in post and the take survives.

For a singer-songwriter who tracks vocals at home and brings stems to a mixer, this mic does what a $1,500+ rig used to do: capture quiet performances cleanly, with no audible noise floor, and with no risk of an unrecoverable clipped take. Reviews from Tom's Hardware, Sound on Sound, and AudioTechnology converge on the same conclusion: the price-to-performance ratio is unusual at this point in the market.

Shure SM7dB — ~$549

The SM7B has been the broadcast and vocal-rock workhorse for decades, but it requires a hot preamp to perform — either an interface with abundant clean gain, or an in-line booster like a Cloudlifter. Shure released the SM7dB with a built-in active preamp: +18 dB or +28 dB selectable boost, phantom-powered (48V from your interface), no batteries.

The capsule is the same as the SM7B's. Reviews at Sound on Sound, MusicRadar, and AudioTechnology consistently note that the integrated preamp produces a cleaner signal chain than running an SM7B through an external Cloudlifter, with fewer cables and noise sources.

If you've been using an SM7B and an external booster, the SM7dB is the mic to upgrade to. If you're shopping at $250, the NT1 5th Gen is the value play. Both mics will make working artists' home recordings sound material better than they did 24 months ago.

STREAMING DASHBOARD — WHAT SPOTIFY 2026 ACTUALLY MEANS

Spotify's January 28, 2026 newsroom post laid out the company's 2026 plans for artists. Three things in there matter for working musicians:

1. The 1,000-stream threshold is here to stay. Tracks that don't reach 1,000 streams within a rolling 12-month window do not generate recorded-music royalties. Spotify says this redirects roughly $40 million annually away from non-music noise uploads and toward music that finds an audience. If you have a deep catalogue of older tracks each generating sub-1,000 annual streams, those tracks no longer pay. (They still count toward audience metrics; they just don't pool royalties.)

2. Editorial expansion is coming. Spotify says it will expand human-led playlists and programming with new mechanisms to give editorial teams a longer window to support emerging artists. Practically, this means human-curated placement may become more recoverable than algorithmic-only paths over the next 12 months — if your pitch-via-Spotify-for-Artists practice has lapsed, restart it.

3. Anti-fraud is being prioritized. Stronger artist verification, song-credit integrity, and identity protection systems are being rolled out across 2026. If you have collaborators on credits, expect more verification friction; if you've ever shared a stage name with another artist, expect to need to clarify it.

The platform paid out a record $11 billion to the music industry in 2025 — the largest annual payment to music from any retailer in history. That's the context for the 2026 changes: the pie is bigger and Spotify is tightening definitions of who eats from it.
