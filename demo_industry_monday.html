INDUSTRY PULSE

BMG and Concord agree to merge, creating a self-described “fourth major”.

On April 28, 2026, BMG and Concord announced a definitive agreement to combine under the BMG name — one of the biggest music mergers in more than a decade. The combined company will be 67% owned by Bertelsmann (BMG's parent) and 33% owned by affiliates of Great Mountain Partners, with a one-time cash payment of $1.16 billion to Great Mountain's affiliates as part of the structure. Bob Valentine, currently CEO of Concord, will serve as CEO of the combined company. Global headquarters will be in Nashville.

The pro-forma EBITDA base for 2026 sits above $730 million, and the merged entity is targeting $1.2 billion in EBITDA over the mid-term. The deal is subject to regulatory approval and is expected to close in the second half of 2026. The combined company will span music publishing, recorded music, theatrical rights, and digital distribution.

Why it matters. The phrase “fourth major” has been used loosely for a decade. This is the first time it has been used by trade press without scare quotes. If the deal closes as announced, the indie tier of the recorded-music business will be structurally different at year-end than it is today — and indie artists currently signed to either parent should be paying close attention to where their catalogues land in the integration.

DEAL RADAR

Sony in advanced talks for Recognition Music Group at $3.5B-$4B.

According to reporting by Bloomberg, Sony Music is in advanced negotiations — through a joint venture with Singapore's sovereign wealth fund GIC — to acquire Recognition Music Group from Blackstone in a deal valued between $3.5 billion and $4 billion. Recognition was formed in March 2025 when Blackstone consolidated its Hipgnosis-era assets under a new brand, and the portfolio holds more than 45,000 songs across over 145 catalogues, including writing and recording rights tied to Justin Bieber, Shakira, 50 Cent, Enrique Iglesias, The Chainsmokers, and Neil Young.

Sony and Blackstone are reportedly pushing to close inside the next seven days as of early May, though sources caution the talks could still collapse. If it closes, this becomes the third multi-billion-dollar music transaction in a matter of months — following the BMG/Concord merger and the sale of Kobalt to Primary Wave Music.

What it signals. Sovereign wealth capital is now a primary buyer in music rights at scale. The market correction of 2023-2024 has clearly ended; multiples on premium catalogues are firming, and the bid-side has more deep pockets than it did 18 months ago.

AI & MUSIC LAB

Warner / Suno: settlement, partnership, and a Songkick handoff.

Warner Music Group and Suno announced their landmark partnership in late November 2025 — settling Warner's copyright lawsuit against Suno and establishing what both parties describe as a first-of-its-kind licensing structure for AI-generated music. The deal also transferred ownership of Songkick, the live-music and concert-discovery platform, from Warner to Suno.

The 2026 platform changes that follow from the deal are worth noting:

  • Suno will launch new, more advanced licensed models in 2026; the current models are slated to be deprecated when the new ones go live.
  • Downloading audio will require a paid account. Free-tier output will be playable and shareable but not downloadable.
  • Paid-tier users will have monthly download caps with the option to pay for additional downloads.
  • Songkick's accumulated dataset — user-level artist preferences, location preferences, alert settings, and years of Spotify-integration history — transfers to Suno alongside the platform.

Universal Music and Sony Music are not party to the Warner-Suno deal and are reportedly seeking copies of the deal's terms as their own AI-licensing negotiations with Suno and Udio continue.

The Songkick angle. The handoff of Songkick's user data to an AI music platform is the part of the deal most under-discussed in the trade press. It hands an AI startup a multi-year, opted-in dataset of fan listening and live-event behaviour — and turns concert discovery into a feature of an AI music product, rather than the other way around.

MONEY MOVES

Spotify: $11 billion paid in 2025, with policy and product changes coming in 2026.

Spotify reported it paid out more than $11 billion to the music industry in 2025, which the company describes as the largest annual payment to music from any retailer in history. Per-stream payouts continue to vary by listener tier and territory: premium streams generate roughly $0.004-$0.005 each, free-tier streams roughly $0.001-$0.002, with platform-wide averages typically reported at $0.003-$0.005.

The 2026 roadmap, per Spotify's January 28 newsroom post, sits on three pillars:

  • Fraud and abuse. Stronger artist verification, song-credit integrity, and identity protection systems are coming in 2026.
  • Editorial. Spotify plans to expand human-led playlists and programming, with new programs designed to give editorial teams a longer-tail mechanism for supporting emerging artists.
  • The 1,000-stream threshold. Tracks must hit at least 1,000 streams in a rolling 12-month window before they generate any recorded-music royalties. Spotify reports this policy redirects roughly $40 million annually away from non-music noise uploads and toward music that finds an audience.

Independent analyst projections suggest 2026 payouts could rise 15-20% over 2025 on the back of subscriber growth and the user-centric payout shift that's been rolling out since 2024 — though these are estimates, not Spotify guidance.

RIGHTS & ROYALTIES

The major-label triangle: Warner is in, Universal and Sony are negotiating.

The three majors signed AI music licensing deals with Klay (a separate AI music startup) in November 2025 — the first sign of an industry-wide pivot from courtroom defence to commercial licensing on AI-generated audio. Warner's settlement with Suno extended that posture to litigation: pay, partner, and structure the upside.

Universal Music and Sony Music remain in active disputes with Suno and Udio, but reporting from Digital Music News in April makes clear that both majors are pushing for visibility into Warner's settlement structure rather than holding the line on no-licensing absolutism. The next 90 days will likely tell us whether the industry settles into a Warner-style posture across the board, or whether Universal and Sony hold out for materially better terms.

For indie artists: watch how mechanical-licensing rates flow through any forthcoming settlements. The 2024 Living Wage for Musicians Act discussion has not generated federal legislation, but DSP-side voluntary changes (Spotify's 1,000-stream threshold, the user-centric payout rollout) are doing some of the work that bill was aiming at — just on platform timing, not regulatory timing.
