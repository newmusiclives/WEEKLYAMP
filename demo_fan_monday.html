NEW MUSIC FRIDAY (THIS THURSDAY) — DRAKE'S ICEMAN

Drake's Iceman drops May 15, 2026. It's his ninth studio album and his first full solo full-length since For All the Dogs in 2023. Released through OVO Sound and Republic Records.

The pre-release singles — “What Did I Miss?”, “Which One” (with Central Cee), and “Dog House” (with Yeat and Julia Wolf) — have already been working on streaming. Expected features on the album proper include Young Thug, 21 Savage, PARTYNEXTDOOR, and Cash Cobain.

The promotional cycle has been characteristic. In April, faux ice appeared on Drake's courtside seats at a Raptors game; downtown Toronto then woke up to a 25-foot ice sculpture with the album's release date hidden inside. The reveal happened on April 21 when streamer Kishka found a magazine and pin-up inside the ice sculpture listing the date.

The song most being parsed in advance is “What Did I Miss?”, where Drake addresses the aftermath of the 2024 feud with Kendrick Lamar — specifically calling out those who he says “played both sides” rather than chose a position. Whether the rest of the album re-litigates that period or moves on is the one open question reviewers have not been able to answer in advance, because nobody outside the camp has heard the full thing yet.

BACKSTAGE PASS — THE RHCP DOC, AND AN AI VOICE

“The Rise of the Red Hot Chili Peppers: Our Brother, Hillel” premiered on Netflix on March 20, 2026. The documentary tells the story of the band's formative years through the lens of original guitarist Hillel Slovak, who died of a heroin overdose in 1988.

The film draws on Hillel's personal journals, previously unseen archival footage, and present-day interviews with Anthony Kiedis, Flea, former Chili Peppers and Pearl Jam drummer Jack Irons, Alain Johannes (Eleven), and George Clinton. So far, so standard for a music documentary.

The unusual choice: Hillel's journal entries are recited by an AI-generated voice trained on recordings of him. The film is, as far as we can determine, the first major music documentary to use generative-AI voice synthesis to give narration to a deceased subject — and the choice is doing exactly the cultural work you'd expect.

The band itself has had a complicated reaction. After initially distancing themselves from the project, Flea told NME (and later Consequence) that the finished documentary “filled my heart right to the top.” Critical response has been mixed: Variety's SXSW review called the film a “blistering portrait of the band's early years” that “melts into an overdone lament.” Roger Ebert's site has it on a separate review page.

The bigger question the film raises — the one that will outlive any specific review — is whether AI voice synthesis of the dead becomes a normalized documentary technique over the next two years, or whether this stays a one-off. The answer probably depends on how Hillel's family and bandmates feel about it twelve months from now, not how critics feel about it today.

STREAMING DASHBOARD — THE $11 BILLION YEAR

Spotify reported on January 28, 2026 that it paid out more than $11 billion to the music industry in 2025 — the largest annual payment to music from any retailer in history. That number tends to read as either reassuring or insulting depending on who you ask, so let's put it in context.

$11 billion is the gross payout to rights-holders (labels, publishers, distributors), not the net cheque to artists. The artist share of that is then determined by the artist's deal with whoever signs them — and the gap between “Spotify paid X to the rights-holder” and “the artist received Y” is the source of most of the friction you've seen in the press over the last decade.

The 2026 changes Spotify announced alongside the number are worth knowing as a fan:

  • Songs that don't reach 1,000 streams in a rolling 12-month window do not earn recorded-music royalties. Spotify says this redirects roughly $40 million annually from non-music noise uploads toward music that finds an audience. Critics argue it disproportionately disadvantages emerging artists.
  • Editorial expansion. Spotify will lean more on human-curated playlists in 2026, partly as a hedge against AI-generated music flooding the catalogue.
  • Anti-fraud measures. Stronger artist verification and song-credit integrity systems are being rolled out across 2026.

If you're a fan and you want more of your subscription dollars to land with the artists you actually listen to, the most direct lever you have is bandcamping, merch-buying, and ticket-buying directly from artists. Streaming is the discovery layer; the support layer is everywhere else.

CONCERT DISCOVERY — SONGKICK NOW LIVES INSIDE AN AI COMPANY

If you ever set up artist alerts on Songkick, this concerns you.

As part of the November 2025 Warner Music / Suno settlement — the deal that ended Warner's copyright lawsuit against the AI music generator — Warner sold Songkick to Suno. The platform continues to operate as a fan-facing concert-discovery tool, but its accumulated dataset (your artist preferences, your location preferences, your alert settings, and years of Spotify-integration history) has been transferred to Suno.

Suno's stated plan is to use the Songkick data to power AI-driven personalization that links its music-creation tools with live-event recommendations. Reporting at Digital Music News and Complete Music Update has called this an ambitious reimagining of concert discovery.

The cleaner read: an AI music platform now owns a multi-year, opted-in fan dataset linking listening behaviour to live-event behaviour. If you want to opt out or export your data, the Songkick site is the place to start — but the data has already moved.
